## **Slide 7**

What is the Verizon Design System? What people are most aware of is our core library of reusable components and the usage guidelines that instruct how to use them. 

The components themselves come in two parts, the Figma component for designers to use when drawing their experiences, and the coded component for engineers to use when building their experiences.

## **Slide 8**

When initially identifying and building our core library of components we  follow the atomic design methodology which simply states that all interfaces can be broken down into atoms and when layered together can create increasingly complex elements.

## **Slide 9**

Our core library consists of atoms and molecules.

## **Slide 13**

While I love what we stand for within Verizon Design, surrounded by passionate and talented designers, it doesn't come without its challenges.

We are very large company and it is difficult for any one person to understand all of the various lines of business that span our enterprise. And all of those lines of business have ongoing projects, all the time. However we recently had an opportunity to plug the design system into a new product launch in a few new ways that reduced friction when integrating the product into our existing ecosystem and enabled greater efficiency within our design iteration process. Leading to a high fidelity product that launched on time and with little error.

## **Slide 14**

Now I'd like to introduce you to myPlan.  
myPlan is a refresh to our Mobile Plans that gives our customers greater flexible and choice in what they want in a phone plan. 

## **Slide 15**

Starting by selecting the data option you want, and then selecting which perks you would like to include which you can go back and change at any time.

This represented a fundamental change in how we structured our plans and how our customers interacted with and selected the plans they wanted. We needed to transform our existing experience.

## **Slide 16**

Even though we were in the process of transforming our Plans experience it is important to remember that this experience does not live in a silo. It lives in an existing ecosystem made up of existing user interfaces that our customers know and understand. 

Our customers understand what our buttons look like and can anticipate what that button will do when it is selected. That builds trust. Even though we need to transform the experience we need to do it in a way that does not confuse or cause our customers to lose trust in our experiences or our Brand. 

Because once this new experience is in front of our customers they need to be able to move through the journey with ease.

## **Slide 17**

Historically for these types of transformational moments, we likely write the brief in a way that enables free thinking and creativity. To try to solve a new problem by thinking outside the box or the constraints inherent within our current toolkit. 

This oftentimes results in a transformational and definitely beautiful experience that when handed off to be built proves to be largely incompatible within the ecosystems it is being built for. 

So things get tweaked and changed in a hurried fashion to the extent that what gets pushed to live is a shell of the original design.

But this time we embedded the design system team into iterative process with the teams creating the designs. This wasn't to stifle the creativity but rather to act as a set of guardrails preventing unnecessary change and enabling the teams to focus on transforming the elements that truly required it.

Here are a few examples.

## **Slide 18**

This first example shows an initial redesign of a plan compare overlay. This is triggered when selecting a plan compare CTA. This new design does mirror our navigation UI however the team did not feel that changing this specific overlay in this specific area made sense holistically within the greater ecosystem. 

So the recommendation was made and accepted to align the overlay with our existing overlay pattern so that it mirrored the overlays that could be encountered both before and after this experience maintaining the uniformity of this common pattern.

## **Slide 19**

This is a more nuanced example showing a couple of selectable tiles. Here the initial design contained much more vertical space and a smaller content header than what is used throughout the ecosystem. Again the recommendation was made and accepted to align these patterns to what is commonly used outside this experience. 

These recommendations were made based on the feeling that this was change for the sake of change and did not actually solve any problems. So the team encouraged transformation where it was needed, and encouraged alignment where it wasn't.

## **Slide 20**

This next example highlights the importance of both alignment to and divergence from the design system. These are new plan select tiles. The teams were solving a new problem here with how to display promotional badging at the top of the tile. 

What was proposed was this badge design with an icon and a red fill color. Also to note is the radio button within the tile that when selected also displays a red fill and a white checkmark. These new interactive patterns were needed but were not reflected in the system.

Contrast that to the primary and secondary buttons at the bottom that are utilizing the standard design system button.

Examples of divergence when it makes sense.

## **Slide 21**

Also to emphasize the value of diverging only when needed is that when you don't and utilize the design system components, you are leveraging all of the user research decisions and testing optimizations that have been built into those elements over the years. As well as Accessibility optimizations. 

This is a quick example of VDS color accessibility and why we only use our red on black when not communicating important or critical information as red on black is inaccessible for certain vision impaired users.

## **Slide 22**

Here's another example where new functionality was created embedding a toggle within a carousel tile within an overlay. Carousels in general are extremely difficult to build accessibly. 

However the design system team had late last year finally achieved success after working closely for months with our Accessibility team. When adding this carousel into the overlay, it came with all of that work already in it and with a few modifications and updates the team was able to make it work in this new scenario.

This further illustrates the value in leverage existing components and then iterating on top of them to create brand new solutions.

I've also deconstructed the overlay on the right showing the various content blocks. Some of these are existing components, some are extended components and some are brand new.

Using a mix of existing with new patterns provides enough holistic uniformity that when this transformed experience is introduced into the broader ecosystem, it fits right in.

## **Slide 23**

Now I am going to pivot from the creative process itself and zoom out to the impact that process had on our partner teams.

Due to the dynamic and sometimes chaotic nature of our environment, teams don't always get the time they need to deliver. I'm going to refer to these as logistical hurdles and we found that by placing the design system in the middle of the design and engineering handoff we were able to remove some of these hurdles.

## **Slide 24**

This is an over simplified depiction of the design iteration process. We have our brief and we get to work. We do exploration, present and get feedback, we test, we refine, and ultimately we hand off a design to be built. 

But as I said this was oversimplified, that single handoff looks a little more like this.

## **Slide 25**

where handoff actually needs to happen across multiple engineering teams that are responsible for individual customer journey ecosystems. 

This happens through a grooming process where the team needs to explain the requirements and designs to each of these teams for them to begin to each individually build out the experiences.

But I said this was oversimplified, because this assumes that our designers were able to get everything drawn and approved in a single design iteration. That the brief they received was immutable and requirements didn't change along the way. That the feedback they needed, they were able to receive within the small window of time they had. 

My history at Verizon has taught me those are bad assumptions to make.

## **Slide 26**

The actual design iteration process looks more like this.... a series of iterations that account for changing requirements, missed communication, and taking the actual time needed to create good design.

However that pencil's down engineering handoff date is still set, and then it is missed. After another iteration, things still aren't ready and it's missed again. 

Missed enough times and you are now in the unfortunate state of having to cut scope because what was finally handed off in infeasible to be built with what remaining time is left.

## **Slide 27**

And remember the handoff needs to happen across multiple engineering teams.

## **Slide 28**

So as I mentioned, this leads to cutting scope. What this could look like on a given page is that certain requirements that were not communicated with enough lead time, or leadership inserting new functionality at the end of the final iteration cycle, or things that just need more time than available have to be cut in favor for more simple or already existing alternatives.

Highlighting the button at the top, that desired action has to be changed. The text in the tiles needs to be pulled from our product database? Well we don't have time to make that connection and adequately test it so it will need to be hard coded. That product image needs to perform some new function? Sorry, no time, it will have to be a static image.

Definitely not an ideal scenario.

## **Slide 29**

This is where we tried something different. We stood up a sub team within the design system engineering team to sit in between the design teams and the engineering teams. This new sub team would create the new organisms and templates needed to deliver the experiences but would build them once and deliver them to all teams as a set of reusable elements.

Shifting the deliverables of the design system from atoms and molecules to also include more complex organisms and even full page templates that all of the teams could consume uniformly.

## **Slide 30**

Here is an example of some of those new elements.

You see the Button and Tile from before, but now we have the Plan select tile as well as the full Plan select page experience that most of the journey ecosystems would leverage.

## **Slide 31**

Having the design system team involved in the design iteration process as well as now creating and handing off more of the experience elements to our engineering partners, allowed for risks to be caught much earlier as well as critical requirements being revealed and captured within the iteration process and be passed on to engineering faster.

These included journey entry and exit requirements, data connection requirements for dynamic content, and new UI functional requirements.

## **Slide 32**

The team was able to create a MVP, a little bit more than a prototype, of the experience while design iteration was still happening, passing pertinent details to engineering that enabled them to preemptively prep their ecosystems for the new experience. 

## **Slide 33**

All while not disrupting the design iteration process that is necessary for getting this new experience right. So our new process somewhat looked like this. Once the MVP was delivered and all teams were able to integrate it into their ecosystems, any subsequent updates that came out of the design iteration process was built into the central set of organisms and templates and pushed to all teams at once.

## **Slide 34**

This enabled our designers to have more time to explore their transformational variations. 

## **Slide 35**

This example demonstrates what was initially shared to our engineering over time. 

Starting with a placeholder, then once data requirements were understood they were able to make those database connections, and when the perks and assets were finalized, it was an easy update to finalize the UI as the backend work had already been completed.

This process also gave us time to care for unexpected changes to requirements.

## **Slide 36**

This is an example of a perk that required a more complex interaction which only became known midway through the project. But given the integration with both design and engineering, there was enough time to care for it.

Each ecosystem launched with a complete and uniform experience.
