### **Three-Paragraph Summary**

Delivered at the 10th Annual Accessibility Summit, "Partnership to Platform" explores the journey of the Verizon Design System (VDS). VDS began as a foundational "people partnership"—a living agreement between designers, engineers, brand managers, and accessibility experts to build inclusive products at scale. By "shifting left" and integrating accessibility reviews into every stage of product design rather than testing at the very end, Verizon made significant strides in ensuring products were "born accessible". However, this human-centric process introduced a new bottleneck: people do not scale well, and the exponential increase in required meetings, reviews, and sign-offs slowed down development when applying updates across the entire system.

To overcome this challenge of scale, the VDS team is engineering an "Intelligent Platform" that automates their partnership. By utilizing "Context Aggregation," the team unifies component documentation, proprietary code, user research, and WCAG 2.1 AA requirements into a single AI "brain". This intelligence is then deployed through "AI-Driven Paired Creating," acting as a proactive co-pilot directly within designers' Figma files and developers' code editors. This shifts the paradigm from reactive audits to proactive compliance, instantly flagging low-contrast colors or suggesting correct ARIA labels during creation. Additionally, AI-driven testing acts as a comprehensive safety net that automates the creation of complex variant test cases, reducing a tedious four-to-six-hour manual task down to mere seconds.

To execute this vision for the next decade, Verizon is implementing a three-pillar framework. First, they will "Empower" teams by providing the necessary AI tools. Second, they will "Train" their workforce in new capabilities, such as prompt engineering, to ensure they can effectively partner with AI. Finally, they will "Aggregate," centralizing and structuring proprietary context so the AI delivers highly specific, Verizon-tailored guidance at the exact right time. Ultimately, the goal of this intelligent platform is not to replace human workers, but to elevate them, freeing up the team to solve the next generation of complex accessibility challenges.

### **Three-Paragraph Summary**

Delivered at UXIndia 2023, "How design systems elevate UX design" explores how Verizon leverages its design system to create experiences that are "Meaningful, Human, and Responsible". While the Verizon Design System (VDS) traditionally provided atomic components and usage guidelines, scaling this across a massive enterprise with constant project demands proved challenging. A major opportunity to rethink this integration arose during the creation of "myPlan," a highly flexible mobile plan experience that needed to seamlessly fit into Verizon's existing digital ecosystem without breaking customer trust.

Historically, transformational design briefs led to creative but largely incompatible experiences that required hurried, scope-cutting compromises before launch. To prevent this during the myPlan project, the VDS team embedded themselves directly into the design iteration process. Instead of stifling creativity, the system acted as guardrails—encouraging designers to align with standard patterns for generic UI elements (like basic overlays) while deliberately diverging from the system to solve brand-new problems (like custom promotional badging). Furthermore, utilizing pre-built VDS components ensured that difficult accessibility requirements, such as complex carousels and strict color contrast rules, were automatically met without needing to be rebuilt from scratch.

Beyond the creative phase, the VDS team also tackled the logistical hurdles of engineering handoff. Handing off complex designs to multiple, distinct engineering teams often leads to missed deadlines and sacrificed features, such as static images replacing dynamic interactive elements. To combat this, VDS stood up a dedicated sub-team to build and deliver full "organisms and templates" instead of just basic components. By supplying an early MVP to engineering teams, backend data connections could be established while front-end design iterations securely continued. Ultimately, centralizing this development resulted in a high-fidelity, uniform launch across all ecosystems with drastically fewer defects, allowing designers to spend less time triaging issues and more time actually designing.
